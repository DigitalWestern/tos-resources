Conservative Treatment

Most TOS cases improve without surgery — if the right conservative approach is used. The community data is surprisingly consistent about which exercises actually move the needle, and the answer is not the PT that most patients are first prescribed.

Can TOS really improve without surgery?

Peer-reviewedCommunity-derived

For most patients with neurogenic TOS, the answer is yes — but not with generic PT. Published literature and community data both support conservative-first treatment, with surgery reserved for patients who either plateau or have structural findings (cervical rib, confirmed compression) that conservative work cannot resolve.

The caveat is that "conservative treatment" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. A generic chain-clinic PT handing you resistance band rows and foam-roller stretches is not the conservative approach that succeeds in recovery stories. The approaches that actually work have three things in common:

  1. They treat TOS as a biomechanical whole-body problem, not an isolated shoulder or neck problem.
  2. They strengthen the scalenes and serratus anterior rather than just stretching tight tissues.
  3. They take months, not weeks, and require patient progression.

Community reality

Multiple Reddit users describe cycling through 2–4 unsuccessful rounds of generic PT before finding a TOS-informed approach that worked. One common pattern: "My PT had me doing chin tucks and rows for four months and I got worse. I switched to serratus and scalene-strengthening work and started improving within weeks."

When conservative treatment is unlikely to be enough

If you have a cervical rib, confirmed arterial or venous compression, progressive neurologic deficit (measurable weakness or muscle atrophy), or a positive diagnostic scalene block but no improvement after 6–12 months of TOS-specific conservative care, it's reasonable to consult a TOS-specialized surgeon. See the Surgery page.

The linchpin: pec minor → serratus → scalenes

Community-derivedClinical reference

The single most actionable insight from the community data is the reciprocal relationship between three muscles: the pec minor, the serratus anterior, and the scalenes. Understanding this chain explains why isolated treatments fail.

The cycle

  1. Pec minor is chronically tight (from forward posture, desk work, stress, or just bad luck).
  2. The tight pec minor inhibits the serratus anterior from firing properly — it essentially holds the scapula in a position where the serratus can't do its job.
  3. With the serratus offline, the scapula doesn't protract and upwardly rotate correctly during arm use.
  4. The neck muscles compensate — scalenes, levator scapulae, and upper trapezius pick up the workload.
  5. Now the scalenes are chronically overloaded, going into protective spasm, compressing the brachial plexus at the interscalene triangle.
  6. The pec minor stays tight because the whole chain is locked. Repeat.
"I found that the pec minor being tight makes the serratus not fire. Which makes your neck do its job which I think starts the issue." — r/ThoracicOutletSupport, Post #4

Why this matters for your protocol

Simply stretching or releasing the pec minor without retraining the serratus will only provide temporary relief — the pec minor tightens back up because the serratus still isn't doing its job. Likewise, strengthening the scalenes without addressing pec minor and serratus misses the upstream cause. The three have to be worked as a system.

Serratus anterior strengthening

Community-derivedPractical tool

This is the single most frequently cited exercise-based intervention in successful conservative recoveries. Multiple users credit serratus work as the breakthrough that resolved their TOS after months of failed PT.

Starter exercises

Wall slides (scaption)
Back against wall, arms in a W shape, slowly slide up and down while keeping contact with the wall. Focus on scapular protraction at the top.
Serratus punches
Lying on back or standing, arm extended forward, "punch" forward by protracting the scapula. No movement at the elbow. 2–3 sets of 10–15 reps.
Plus push-ups
At the top of a push-up (or wall / incline push-up if full is too much), push further to protract the scapulae. The "plus" is what activates the serratus.
Bear-crawl serratus hold
Quadruped position with knees hovering. Actively push the floor away to protract both scapulae. Hold 15–30 seconds.

Cue to focus on

You should feel the work across the side of the ribcage, not in the shoulder joint or traps. If you feel it in the upper traps, drop the intensity and re-cue.

Scalene strengthening (not stretching)

Community-derivedAnecdotal / caution

The MSK Neurology / Kjetil Larsen approach is the most-referenced specific protocol in the dataset. The core principle is counterintuitive: rather than stretching tight scalenes, you strengthen them through controlled isometric and eccentric loading.

The rationale: weak, deconditioned scalenes go into protective spasm, which compresses the brachial plexus. Strengthening them allows them to relax at rest. Stretching alone, especially aggressive stretching, can inflame an already-irritated muscle.

The starter protocol

  • Starting position: Side-lying on a bench with a book (about 2.5cm / 1 inch) supporting the head to limit end-range neck side-bend.
  • Movement: Slow, controlled lateral neck flexion — lifting the head laterally off the book, returning with control.
  • Volume to start: 8 reps, twice per week. That is intentionally low.
  • Progression: Very gradual over weeks to months. Add reps before adding range of motion. Add range of motion before adding resistance.

This is the easiest exercise to mess up

One community member overdid scalene strengthening in the first month and inflamed the muscles significantly, setting them back weeks. Start conservatively and progress slowly. Temporary soreness 24 hours after is fine; lingering symptom flare is a signal to back off.

If you have acute inflammation or recent flare, do not start strengthening — address the flare first (rest, ice, anti-inflammatories) and restart conservatively.

Full protocol reference

The specific progression, imagery, and full exercise library are maintained at mskneurology.com (Kjetil Larsen). It is the single most-referenced conservative TOS resource in the community. A TOS-specialized PT familiar with this work is ideal.

Pec minor release

Community-derivedPractical tool

Pec minor release is one of the highest-leverage and lowest-risk self-treatments in the dataset. Done daily alongside serratus strengthening, it breaks the chain described above.

Techniques

Cork ball self-release
12cm cork massage ball placed under the armpit/chest wall near the pec minor. Lie on it, let body weight apply sustained pressure. 2–5 minutes per side. Repeat daily. Multiple community members report this as transformative.
Bench passive stretch
Lie flat on a bench, arms hanging laterally straight left and right, holding 5–10 lb dumbbells. Let gravity pull the pec minor into length. 1–2 minutes. Do not force.
Active doorway stretch
Arm at 90° against a doorframe, slight lean forward. Combine with deep diaphragmatic breathing to expand the ribcage into the stretch. 30–60 seconds.
Deep ribcage breathing
Expanding the ribcage laterally on inhale counteracts pec minor tightness. Particularly useful during anxiety/panic flares, which tighten the pec minor further and can trigger symptoms.
Rolfing / deep tissue
Manual therapy targeting the pec minor specifically. One user reports Rolfing as a breakthrough, especially alongside home self-release.

If you have shoulder instability, go carefully

The pec minor is a major shoulder stabilizer. Aggressive release in someone with underlying shoulder instability (e.g. labral tear, hypermobility) can worsen shoulder symptoms. One community member reports worsened instability after surgical pec minor release. If you have shoulder laxity, start very gently and pair release with rotator cuff stability work.

Posture: shoulders up, not back and down

Community-derivedAnecdotal / caution

This is a somewhat controversial but repeatedly successful finding in community data. Multiple users report that the standard PT cue — "shoulders back and down" — worsens TOS by depressing the clavicle into the costoclavicular space. Instead, keeping the shoulders slightly elevated opens the thoracic outlet.

The practical cue is "tall neck, shoulders slightly lifted, chest open" — not forced, just neutral-to-slightly-elevated rather than actively pulled down.

Important caveat by compression site

The "shoulders up" cue primarily helps costoclavicular compression (between clavicle and first rib). For interscalene triangle compression, elevating the shoulders can actually increase scalene tension. If you have confirmed interscalene involvement, you likely need a more nuanced cue — elevating the shoulder girdle while keeping scalene tone low. A TOS-informed PT can dial this in.

Whole-body mechanics

Community-derivedClinical reference

The most detailed recovery stories in the dataset consistently emphasize that TOS cannot be solved by treating only the neck and shoulder. The thoracic outlet sits on top of a kinetic chain, and problems downstream show up as compression upstream.

Downstream contributors worth assessing

  • Anterior pelvic tilt — shifts the ribcage forward and rotates it, directly affecting the thoracic outlet. Correcting pelvic position changes upstream mechanics.
  • Hip flexor / psoas tightness — contributes to the postural cascade; one user specifically credits hip flexor work.
  • Weak core — allows compensatory patterns at the shoulder and neck. Diaphragmatic breathing and transverse abdominis work help.
  • Ribcage expansion asymmetry — PRI (Postural Restoration Institute) approach targets the inability to fully expand the affected side's ribcage.
  • Leg length discrepancy — one community member's shoulders aligned properly only after addressing a real leg length difference with a heel lift.

Practitioners to look up

Zac Cupples and Bill Hartman are the most-referenced PRI-adjacent practitioners in the community. Their YouTube content explains the full-body mechanics approach well — worth watching before committing to any PT plan.

Counterstrain PT — the surprising outlier

Counterstrain (a gentle, indirect osteopathic technique) is cited by multiple community members as uniquely effective compared to standard PT. It does not look like typical exercise-based PT — it involves positioning joints and muscles in their pain-free range and holding to allow involuntary release. Worth asking about if you can find a counterstrain-trained practitioner.

Nerve glides

Community-derivedPractical tool

Nerve glides (neural mobilization) — slow, controlled movements that slide the nerve through its sheath without stretching it — reduce the severity of flares for many community members when done consistently. For ulnar nerve involvement (the most common in nTOS), ulnar glides are the most relevant.

Flossing vs. tensioning

There is an important distinction: nerve flossing (the nerve slides with neutral tension on both ends) is generally well-tolerated. Nerve tensioning (both ends held in stretch simultaneously) is more aggressive and can inflame an already-irritated nerve. If gentle glides flare your symptoms for more than a day, stop and reassess with your PT. This is why one of the exercise tracker versions omits nerve flossing entirely.

Finding a TOS-specialized PT

Community-derivedPractical tool

Across almost every detailed recovery story in the dataset, one factor shows up repeatedly: the quality and specialization of the PT. General PTs and chain clinics are consistently described as inadequate for TOS. Multiple users describe worsening during generic PT and only improving after finding someone who understood the mechanics.

What to look for

  • Familiarity with the MSK Neurology / Kjetil Larsen approach, or PRI training, or counterstrain training.
  • Experience with TOS specifically — not just "necks and shoulders."
  • Willingness to progress slowly and adjust based on your symptoms.
  • Understanding that stretching tight scalenes is usually the wrong first move.
  • An assessment of downstream mechanics (pelvis, ribcage, breathing), not just the neck.

Questions to ask a prospective PT

  • How many TOS patients have you treated?
  • What's your approach to scalene involvement — do you strengthen or stretch?
  • Are you familiar with Kjetil Larsen's work or PRI?
  • How do you distinguish TOS-driven symptoms from cervical radiculopathy in treatment?
  • What's your view on "shoulders back and down"?

A negative answer is still useful

If a PT says they'll have you do rows, chin tucks, and pec stretches as the first-line approach, they're not wrong about those being real exercises — but if they're your entire program and the PT has no specific answer about serratus retraining or downstream mechanics, the community data says you're unlikely to see much change.

Sleep, ergonomics, and daily load

Community-derivedPractical tool

Exercise work happens once a day. The other 23 hours — how you sleep, sit, type, and carry things — determine whether that work sticks or gets undone.

Sleep

  • MedCline pillow (body pillow + wedge with arm cutout) is cited by multiple community members as the single best sleep upgrade.
  • Sleep on the non-affected side with the affected arm supported forward, not compressed underneath.
  • Avoid sleeping on your back with arms overhead — classic position for reproducing symptoms at night.

Workstation

  • Arm rests — keep shoulders supported and slightly elevated, not hanging.
  • Split keyboard and vertical mouse — reduces the internal rotation and forward reach that tightens pec minor.
  • Monitor at eye level — forward head posture crushes the thoracic outlet; look forward, not down.
  • Voice-to-text — multiple community members switch to dictation to reduce arm load; macOS and iOS have usable dictation built in.
  • Timer-based movement breaks — every 30–45 minutes, stand, reset posture, do 30 seconds of active mobility.

Daily load

  • Backpacks, not shoulder bags — and loaded on both straps, not one.
  • Carry groceries in a cart or split between both arms; avoid single heavy load on the affected side.
  • Heat helps nerve pain specifically — hot baths, heating pads on the neck/shoulder region.
  • Ice at night helps some users; others find it aggravating. Experiment.

A realistic timeline

Community-derivedPractical tool

Conservative TOS treatment is a months-to-years process, not weeks. Expectations matter — many users who "failed PT" in six weeks actually just hadn't done it long enough with the right protocol.

Weeks 1–4
Establish baseline. Start daily pec minor release, deep breathing. Find a TOS-informed PT. Get a diagnostic scalene block if you haven't.
Weeks 4–12
Begin serratus retraining (wall slides, punches, bear-crawl holds). Start scalene strengthening at very low volume. Begin addressing downstream mechanics (pelvis, ribcage).
Months 3–6
Gradual progression of scalene and serratus load. Integrate breathing/PRI work. Baseline tightness should start decreasing. If not, reassess protocol and practitioner.
Months 6–12
Consolidation. Many conservative recoveries describe noticeable functional improvement here — not necessarily pain-free, but able to do more without flaring.
Year 1+
If conservative treatment has truly plateaued with no functional gains over 3–6 months, consider surgical consultation — not necessarily for surgery, but to understand your options.

Flares are normal

Recovery is not linear. Expect occasional flares — after overuse, stress, illness, or sometimes for no clear reason. A flare is not a failure of the protocol; it's data about your current tolerance. Back off, recover, and return to the work.
